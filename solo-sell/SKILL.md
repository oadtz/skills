# Solo — Sell (founder-led, first paying customers)

Take attention (from `solo-distribute`) and an offer + price (from `solo-model`) and **turn them into
closed, paying deals by selling personally.** Output is not a theory of sales — it's a concrete sales
motion the founder runs this week: who to talk to, what to say, how to run the conversation, how to
handle the price objection, and how to capture what works into a repeatable playbook.

## The governing principle (read first)

**For a solo founder, early sales is not "convincing" — it is *learning sold one conversation at a
time*; you sell by understanding, and you systematize only what repeats.**

The first customers don't come from a funnel or a hire — they come from the founder personally talking
to people with the problem. Two truths shape everything here, and they're two halves of one idea:

1. **Selling is learning, not pitching.** Every outbound message is a *hypothesis test*, every early
   call is *fieldwork* (Jen Abel). You're not there to convince — you're there to find out whether the
   pain, the buyer, and the offer actually line up, and to let what you learn reshape the offer. This
   is the Mom Test applied to selling: ask about their world and their past behavior, talk less, and
   trust what they *do* (sign, pre-pay, pilot) over what they *say*. A founder who listens out-sells a
   founder who pitches.
2. **The signal is repeatability, not revenue.** One closed deal can be luck; the goal of founder-led
   sales is a *motion you can repeat*. By deal 5 you should recognize the objection from deal 1 and
   have a written answer; by deal 10 your discovery call has no new questions. *That* — not a revenue
   number — is the sign it's working and the moment a playbook exists. Only then is anything worth
   automating or (much later) handing to someone else.

## Where this sits in the pipeline

```
solo-model → solo-fund → solo-distribute → solo-sell (THIS) → solo-grow → solo-sustain
            offer+price      get attention      close the deal      automate    run it solo
```

`solo-sell` is the **conversion** step — it closes the buyers `solo-distribute` attracts using the
offer and price `solo-model` set. It runs tightly with both: weak demand → back to `solo-distribute`;
the offer/price itself is wrong (not just the pitch) → back to `solo-model`.

- If the user has **no audience / no one to talk to yet**, send them to `solo-distribute` first — you
  can't sell into an empty room.
- If the user hasn't **decided the offer or price**, send them to `solo-model` — sell needs something
  concrete to sell.
- This stage ends when the user has closed (or pre-sold) their first customers **and** has the start of
  a repeatable sales motion written down.

## When founder-led sales is the right motion

This skill assumes the founder should be doing sales personally. That's correct when: it's B2B (or
high-consideration B2C), the deal is worth roughly **$5K+ in annual value**, the business is **below
~$1M ARR**, and there's **no established playbook** for the wedge yet. If the product is low-price,
high-volume, self-serve (e.g. a $9/mo app), the motion is *distribution + self-serve checkout*, not
sales calls — route back to `solo-distribute` and keep `solo-model`'s self-serve pricing.

## Host capability mapping

- **User input**: ask one concise question at a time; use structured choices if the host supports them.
- **Research**: use the host's web/search/browser to research specific prospects/accounts and comparable
  deals; use connected CRM/email/calendar to pull real pipeline, past threads, and meetings if available.
- **No live research**: work from the user's described ICP and prospects; mark prospect-fit and
  willingness-to-pay as hypotheses to test in real conversations.
- **Drafting**: when asked, draft the actual outreach message, call agenda, follow-up, or pre-sell
  offer — concrete and ready to send, in the user's voice.
- **File output**: write the sales motion / playbook to a file/artifact if supported; else deliver in chat.

**Grounding:** before stating any external fact (comparable deal sizes, "what buyers pay", market
benchmarks), follow `../solo-grounding.md` if present — source-or-tag every claim and re-fetch
time-sensitive numbers. It does not make you less decisive about the ask you tell the founder to make.

## Workflow

Five steps: **Pin the ICP & target list → Reach out as hypothesis tests → Run the conversation (Mom
Test) → Close / pre-sell with the offer → Capture the repeatable playbook.**

### Step 1 — Pin the ICP and a real target list

Narrow from "who could buy" to **who to talk to this week**. Define a sharp ICP (the specific person +
trigger that makes the pain urgent now), then list **10–20 named, reachable prospects** — real people
or accounts, not a segment. The first customers should be the ones who feel the pain *most* and are
*easiest to reach*, not the most prestigious logo. Pull from the audience `solo-distribute` built and
any warm edges first.

### Step 2 — Reach out as hypothesis tests, not pitches

Read `references/founder-led-sales.md`. Each outbound message is a **test of a specific hypothesis**
(this buyer, this pain, this trigger) — short, specific, about *them*, asking for a conversation, not
pitching the product. Aim for a small batch you can personalize, not a blast. Track which message
framing earns replies; that signal reshapes both the targeting and the offer.

### Step 3 — Run the conversation by the Mom Test

The early call is **fieldwork**, not a demo. Lead with their world: how they handle the problem today,
what it costs them, when it last bit them, what they've already tried or paid for. **Talk less than
half the time.** Anchor on past behavior and real spend, not hypotheticals ("would you buy?" is
useless). Only demo/pitch once you understand the pain well enough to connect the offer to it in their
words. Capture objections and the exact language they use — it's the raw material for the playbook and
for sharpening the offer.

### Step 4 — Close or pre-sell with the offer

Make a clear ask. For an unbuilt or early product, **pre-selling is the strongest validation** — money
up front (deposit, pilot fee, founding-customer price, LOI with a date) proves the pain far better than
"that's interesting." Use the offer and price from `solo-model`; if the deal keeps stalling on price,
diagnose honestly whether it's a *pitch* problem (Step 3) or an *offer/price* problem (the value, the
risk-reversal, or the price level is wrong → loop back to `solo-model`, don't just discount reflexively).
Handle the common objections with prepared, honest responses (see the reference); reverse risk with a
guarantee or a small paid pilot where it genuinely lowers the buyer's risk.

### Step 5 — Capture the repeatable playbook

After each deal, write down what happened: the message that got the reply, the questions that surfaced
the pain, the objection and the answer that worked, why they bought or didn't. By ~deal 5–10 these
notes *are* the sales playbook. Then present it:

> **ICP & trigger**: [who, and what makes it urgent now]
> **Target list**: [the next 10–20 real prospects]
> **Outreach that works**: [the message framing earning replies]
> **Discovery**: [the 3–5 questions that reliably surface the pain]
> **Top objections → answers**: [objection: response] (esp. price)
> **The ask**: [pre-sell / pilot / close — exact offer + price from solo-model]
> **Repeatability check**: [are deals starting to rhyme? if not, what's still random?]
>
> Want me to draft the next batch of outreach, or — once this motion is repeating — hand to
> **solo-grow** to systematize the repeatable part into an automatic, scalable engine (self-serve
> funnel, growth loop, unit economics)? Or **solo-sustain** to keep it runnable without burning out? If
> demand is the problem, back to **solo-distribute**; if the offer/price is the problem, **solo-model**.

The user owns the relationships and the calls; this skill arms them and keeps them honest.

## Operating principles

- **Sell by listening.** Talk less than half the time; the founder who understands the pain best wins
  the deal. Pitching at people is the amateur move.
- **Behavior over words (Mom Test).** A pre-payment, a signed pilot, a calendar hold beat any amount of
  "I'd definitely use this." Chase commitments, not compliments.
- **Pre-sell before you build.** For early/unbuilt products, money up front is the cleanest proof the
  problem is real — and the cheapest way to avoid building the wrong thing.
- **Do things that don't scale — on purpose.** Manual, high-touch, bespoke first deals are correct;
  they teach you the motion. Systematize only what has repeated, never before.
- **A stalled deal is information.** Diagnose whether it's pitch, demand, or offer/price — and route to
  the right skill — instead of reflexively discounting (which trains the market to wait for a discount).
- **Don't hire sales to escape selling.** The founder must close the first ~10–20 deals and write the
  playbook before anyone else can sell; outsourcing this too early buys noise, not revenue.
- **Honesty:** never oversell or manufacture urgency that isn't real — it wins a bad-fit customer who
  churns and costs founder-hours later (see `solo-sustain`). If the honest answer is "this isn't a fit
  for them," say so and move to the next prospect. Mark willingness-to-pay as a hypothesis until real
  money confirms it.

## Execution

**Don't stop at the plan — execute it.** Build the real deliverables with the right tool (draft the
actual outreach emails, call agenda, objection-response doc, and pre-sell offer in the founder's voice)
instead of handing back advice. Every outreach draft passes `../solo-craft.md`: no cold-email centroid
("Quick question", fake personalization, the reflexive 15-minute-call ask), each message anchored to a
real prospect detail, and the read-aloud test — if the founder wouldn't say it to the prospect's face,
cut it. Stage anything that *sends* from the founder's accounts or commits them
for their one-click approval — you draft, they hit send. Full contract: `../solo-execution.md`.
