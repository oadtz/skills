# Founder-led sales — the motion in detail

This is the "how" behind `solo-sell`. The spine: **early sales is learning sold one conversation at a
time, and the goal is a *repeatable motion*, not a revenue number.** None of this is a script to read
at people — it's a way to run real conversations that simultaneously close deals and sharpen the offer.

## When this motion applies (and when it doesn't)

Founder-led, conversation-based selling is right when **B2B (or high-consideration B2C), deal value
~$5K+/yr, below ~$1M ARR, and no established playbook** for the wedge. Below that — a low-price,
high-volume, self-serve product — the "motion" is distribution + a checkout button, not sales calls;
stay in `solo-distribute` and `solo-model`. Don't run enterprise-style sales on a $9/mo product, and
don't expect self-serve to close a $50k deal.

## 1. ICP and the target list

- **Sharpen the ICP to a person + a trigger,** not a segment. "Mid-market data-center developers" is a
  segment; "the person who owns site feasibility, right after a site fails a water review" is an ICP
  with a trigger. The trigger is what makes the pain *urgent now*.
- **List 10–20 named, reachable prospects.** Real names/accounts you can actually reach this week —
  start with the warmest edges and the audience `solo-distribute` built. Prestige logos are a trap
  early; pick whoever feels the pain most and answers fastest.

## 2. Outreach as hypothesis tests

Each message tests one hypothesis (this buyer + this pain + this trigger). Keep it short, specific,
about *them*, and ask for a conversation — not the sale.

```
Subject: [specific to their world, not your product]

Hi [name] — saw [specific trigger/signal about them]. I work with [their kind of role] on
[the pain, in their language], and I'm trying to understand how you handle [X] today.
Not selling anything — would 15 minutes this week be worth it to compare notes?
```

- **Personalize a small batch; don't blast.** Reply rate *is* the test result — if a framing earns
  replies, that pain/buyer hypothesis is warm; if silence, change the hypothesis, not the volume.
- **In 2025+, you can offload top-of-funnel grunt work** (list-building, research) to AI tools or a
  freelance SDR — but the founder runs the actual conversations. Don't outsource the learning.

## 3. The discovery call — run it by the Mom Test

The first call is **fieldwork**, not a demo.

- **Talk less than half the time.** Your job is to understand, not to impress.
- **Ask about their world and their past behavior,** never hypotheticals:
  - "Walk me through the last time [problem] happened — what did you do?"
  - "How are you handling this today? What does that cost you (time/money/risk)?"
  - "Have you tried to solve it before? What did you buy or build? What happened?"
  - "Who else is affected? Whose budget would this come from?"
- **Avoid the questions that produce lies:** "Would you use this?", "Do you like this idea?", "Would
  you pay $X?" — people say yes to be nice. Trust what they've *done* and what they'll *commit* to.
- **Demo only after you understand the pain** well enough to connect the offer to it in *their* words.
- **Write down the exact language** they use for the problem — it becomes your copy, your offer, and
  your objection answers.

## 4. Close or pre-sell — get a commitment, not a compliment

The ask should be proportional to what you've learned. For early/unbuilt products, **pre-selling is the
strongest signal there is:**

- **Forms of real commitment (strongest → weakest):** cash up front (deposit / founding-customer price)
  → signed pilot with a fee → paid pilot → LOI with a date and success criteria → a scheduled next step
  with the economic buyer. "Send me more info" and "let's reconnect next quarter" are *not* commitments.
- **Pilots / design partners.** A small, paid, time-boxed engagement that co-builds the v1 with a real
  customer is ideal early: it funds the build, proves demand with money, and produces a reference. Keep
  it bounded (scope + end date) so it doesn't become unpaid consulting forever.
- **Make the ask explicit and once.** State the offer and price (from `solo-model`) plainly, then stop
  talking. Silence after the ask is the founder's hardest and most important skill.

## 5. The price objection — diagnose before you discount

"It's too expensive" is usually not about the number. Diagnose:

- **Value not landed?** They don't yet see the outcome being worth it → back to discovery; re-anchor on
  the cost of the problem (Step 3), not features. (This is the Hormozi value lever — raise perceived
  value rather than cut price; see `solo-model`.)
- **Risk too high?** They believe the value but doubt *they'll* get it → reverse risk: a guarantee, a
  paid pilot, a milestone-based start. Lower the risk, not the price.
- **Wrong buyer / no budget?** You're talking to someone who can't say yes → find the economic buyer.
- **Genuinely mispriced?** If many qualified buyers stall on price for the same reason, the *offer or
  price itself* may be wrong → loop back to `solo-model`. This is an offer problem, not a pitch problem.
- **Reflexive discounting is a trap.** Cutting price on request trains the market to wait for the
  discount, attracts the most demanding/least loyal customers, and signals the price was fake. Hold the
  price; change the value, the risk, or the buyer.

## Common objections → honest responses

- **"Send me some info."** → "Happy to — but so I send the *right* thing, can I ask two quick questions
  about how you handle [X] today?" (turns a brush-off back into discovery).
- **"We already use [incumbent]."** → "Makes sense — what does it not do well for [their specific
  situation]?" (find the gap; don't bash the incumbent).
- **"Not a priority right now."** → "Totally fair — what *is* on fire this quarter?" (qualify out
  honestly, or surface the real trigger).
- **"It's too expensive."** → diagnose per above; never auto-discount.
- **"Let me think about it."** → "Of course — what's the one thing you'd need to be true to move
  forward?" (surface the real objection instead of a slow no).

## How you know it's working: repeatability

The signal is **not** the first dollar — it's the *motion starting to rhyme*:

- By **deal 5**, you recognize the objection from deal 1 and have a written answer.
- By **deal 10**, the discovery call surfaces no new questions; you can predict the path.
- The same outreach framing keeps earning replies; the same 3–5 questions keep surfacing the pain.

When that happens, you have a **playbook** — write it down. Only now is it worth automating parts or
(much later, and only with durable revenue) handing sales to anyone else. Before repeatability,
"scaling sales" just scales randomness. Founder closes the first ~10–20; the playbook is the deliverable.
