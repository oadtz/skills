# The funding cost ladder for solo founders

The principle: *keep the cap table and obligations as empty as the business allows.* So rank every
option by **cost to the founder** — control given up + obligations created + strings attached — and
climb only when a specific need justifies the next rung.

> Terms, eligibility, and the very existence of named programs change constantly. Always verify
> current details from the source before acting. Nothing here is financial, investment, tax, or legal
> advice — for a specific deal, read the actual agreement and consult a qualified professional.

## Contents

- Rungs 0-2: bootstrapping, pre-sales, grants, and credits

## Rung 0 — Bootstrapping (default)
Founder's savings + reinvested revenue.
- **Cost:** lowest possible — no equity, no repayment, no strings. **Requires:** personal runway and a
  model that can start earning. **Fits:** every model. **Watch-out:** undercapitalizing your *own*
  living costs; budget founder runway honestly or burnout/forced-quit follows (see `solo-sustain`).

## Rung 1 — Pre-sales & crowdfunding (non-dilutive + validation)
Presell the product, lifetime/founding-member deals, Kickstarter/Indiegogo, paid waitlists.
- **Cost:** none in equity; you owe *delivery* of what you promised. **Requires:** something concrete
  enough to promise and an audience to sell to (hand audience-building to `solo-distribute`).
- **Why it's often the smartest first "raise":** money and proof of demand at the same time, with zero
  dilution. A failed pre-sale is cheap, fast validation that saved you the build.

## Rung 2 — Grants, competitions & platform/AI credits (non-dilutive cash + free runway)
Government, foundation, university, and platform startup funds; pitch competitions. In Thailand, for
example: **depa** (digital economy), **NIA** (innovation), **NSTDA** programs. In the US, the big one is
**SBIR/STTR** — federal non-dilutive R&D grants (reported ~$4B+/yr, reauthorized through 2031 per
[spectup](https://www.spectup.com/resource-hub/non-dilutive-funding)) — plus state innovation grants.
- **Cost:** no equity, but real *time* — applications, reporting, eligibility criteria, sometimes
  matching-fund or local-entity requirements. **Requires:** fitting the program's theme/eligibility.
- **Watch-out:** slow; don't let grant-chasing become procrastination disguised as funding.
- **Platform & AI compute credits (effectively free runway, era-specific):** cloud/AI programs now give
  serious non-dilutive credit you can *stack* — e.g. AWS Activate (up to ~$300K GenAI tier), Google for
  Startups (up to ~$350K AI-first), Azure Founders Hub (up to ~$150K), NVIDIA Inception. For an AI-heavy
  build these extend runway more than a small raise would, and stacking is the norm, not the exception
  ([VC Corner](https://www.thevccorner.com/p/ai-startup-credits-guide)). *Magnitudes/tiers change and the
  top tiers often require accelerator/VC affiliation — verify each.*
- **Web3 / public-goods route (niche):** if you build open-source or web3 public goods, **retroactive
  funding** (Optimism RetroPGF — 60M+ OP distributed) and **quadratic funding** (Gitcoin — $56M+) are
  real non-dilutive sources ([Gitcoin](https://gitcoin.co/)) — but *only* for that specific kind of
  builder; ignore it otherwise.

## Rung 3 — Invoice financing / factoring (non-dilutive, needs receivables)
Borrow against unpaid B2B invoices — turn 30/60/90-day receivables into cash now (factoring / AR finance).
- **Cost:** no equity; a fee/discount on each invoice, cleared as the customer pays. **Requires:** real
  B2B invoices/receivables. **Fits:** services/B2B with slow-paying clients (enterprise/government
  contracts especially); **mismatched** to pre-revenue or consumer one-offs.
- **Use it as:** a cashflow *bridge* that smooths lumpy receivables — not growth capital, and not a way
  to fund a zero-revenue build.

## Rung 4 — Revenue-based financing (RBF) (debt-like, no equity)
Capital repaid as a fixed % of monthly revenue until a cap (e.g. 1.3–1.5×) is hit. Providers exist for
SaaS/subscription and e-commerce.
- **Cost:** no equity, but an obligation serviced from revenue — it eats cashflow precisely when you
  may need it. **Requires:** *existing, fairly predictable* revenue. **Fits:** MicroSaaS / recurring;
  **mismatched** to pre-revenue or lumpy one-off-product income.
- **Use it as:** an accelerant once something already works — not a way to fund a zero-revenue build.

## Rung 5 — Revenue/ARR-backed credit facilities & venture debt (debt; mild/no equity)
Borrow against recurring revenue at scale: MRR/ARR-backed credit facilities (e.g. Capchase/Arc/Lighter
Capital — reportedly up to ~70% of ARR, funded in days), or, for already-funded companies, venture debt.
- **Cost:** facilities ~9–11% all-in; venture debt ~12–15% all-in *including warrants* (mild dilution).
  An obligation serviced from revenue — a slow month still owes. **Requires:** several months of steady
  MRR (often ~$10–15k+ MRR). **Fits:** recurring-revenue businesses past product-market fit.
- **Vs. RBF (Rung 4):** RBF is smaller, simpler, and pricier (often 12–25% annualized); these facilities
  are larger, more institutional, and cheaper — reportedly now below a bridge SAFE's true cost for
  revenue-stage founders ([non-dilutive 2026](https://capwave.ai/blog/blog-non-dilutive-financing-2026)).
  *Point-in-time; verify current terms.*

## Rung 6 — Shared-earnings agreements (SEAL) (bootstrapper-designed)
Pioneered for bootstrappers (e.g. Earnest-Capital-style). Investor puts in capital (historically on
the order of low-to-mid six figures) repaid as a share of **profit before founder salary**, with a
capped buy-back that converts to a small residual equity stake if you never pay it all back.
- **Cost:** between debt and equity — you share upside but can buy most of it back; no forced exit.
  **Requires:** a real or near-term-profitable business and an investor relationship. **Fits:**
  founders who want to keep control and don't plan a classic VC exit.

## Rung 7 — Bootstrapper accelerators (e.g. TinySeed-style)
Remote accelerators built for bootstrapped SaaS: modest capital (historically ~$120k for the first
founder, less per additional founder) for a **small permanent equity stake** (historically ~10–12%),
plus a year-ish of mentorship and a peer cohort.
- **Cost:** permanent minority equity + program commitment. **Requires:** SaaS with real growth
  potential (these target 7–8-figure outcomes, **not** lifestyle-scale businesses). **Fits:** a solo
  SaaS founder who wants growth + community and will trade some equity for it.

## Rung 8 — Equity crowdfunding (Reg CF / Reg A+) (dilutive, from the crowd)
Sell equity to many small investors via a regulated platform — **Reg CF** (up to $5M) or **Reg A+**
(larger); WeFunder / StartEngine / DealMaker / Republic. The combined Reg CF + Reg A+ market raised
~$925M in 2025 ([Kingscrowd](https://kingscrowd.com/regulation-crowdfunding-through-april-2025/)).
- **Cost:** real dilution — *but* no single VC gatekeeper or board control. The downside is a cap table
  of many small shareholders + compliance, and the raise *is itself a marketing campaign* you must run.
  **Requires:** a consumer-relatable story and an audience to mobilize. **Fits:** consumer/brand
  businesses with a crowd; **mismatched** to a quiet B2B solo tool with no narrative.
- **Where it sits:** dilutive, so high on the ladder — but lower control-loss than VC. *Point-in-time
  figures; verify platform terms and current limits.*

## Rung 9 — Priced equity: angels & VC (most expensive in control)
Selling ownership at a valuation; SAFEs/notes that convert to equity.
- **Cost:** highest — permanent dilution **plus** an imported mandate to grow fast and exit. A single
  round reshapes what the business is *allowed* to become. **Requires:** a venture-scale plan and
  appetite for that path. **Fits:** rarely a true solo/lifestyle business; appropriate only when the
  founder genuinely wants venture scale.

## Market context (point-in-time — verify before acting)

The non-dilutive menu has widened as VC tightened — useful tailwind for a solo founder who wants to
avoid the cap table:

- **Revenue-based financing is growing fast** — reported ~62%/yr growth, ~$9.77B volume in 2025
  ([ecaplabs](https://www.ecaplabs.com/blogs/revenue-based-financing)). More providers = better terms
  for recurring-revenue solo businesses (Rungs 4–5).
- **The big era shift:** AI has slashed build costs, so the question moves from "how much to raise" to
  "do you even need to?" — stack free AI/cloud credits for runway (Rung 2) and borrow against revenue
  (Rungs 4–5) rather than sell equity. The zero-interest, hockey-stick-deck era is over; capital now
  flows to non-dilutive instruments tied to real revenue.
- **Mix and match by stage, don't pick one.** The modern playbook layers sources: bootstrap + pre-sales
  early, grants/credits where eligible, RBF once revenue is steady, equity only if genuinely
  venture-scale ([spectup non-dilutive guide](https://www.spectup.com/resource-hub/non-dilutive-funding)).
- *These figures are point-in-time from this research; re-check before relying on them, and never
  present a remembered rate or program as a current fact (see `../solo-grounding.md`).*

## Choosing
1. Can you bootstrap it? Then do (and stop here).
2. Is there something to pre-sell? Pre-sales beat any external raise — money + validation, no dilution.
3. Non-dilutive grants + **stack free platform/AI credits** before anything dilutive.
4. B2B with slow-paying invoices? **Invoice financing** to bridge cashflow (not to fund the build).
5. Steady recurring revenue and need an accelerant? **RBF** (small/simple) → **MRR-backed credit
   facility / venture debt** (bigger, cheaper) — recurring models only.
6. Want a partner but not a VC exit? **SEAL**.
7. Venture-scale SaaS and want growth + cohort? **Accelerator**.
8. Consumer-relatable story and a crowd to mobilize? **Equity crowdfunding** — dilutive, but no single
   gatekeeper.
9. Genuinely venture-scale and want it? **Priced equity** — eyes open about the mandate it imports.

In every case the test is the same: *the lowest rung that clears the specific, named need.*
