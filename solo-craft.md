# Solo — Craft bar (customer-facing words that don't smell like a bot)

Read this before drafting anything a customer or prospect will actually read: outreach messages,
build-in-public posts, landing/funnel copy, lifecycle emails, offers. `solo-grounding.md` keeps the
*facts* honest; this file keeps the *words* human. The failure it counters: technically correct copy
that every prospect has already deleted a hundred times this month — the AI cold-email and AI
landing-page centroid. Deliverability and reply rates die on this, so it's a revenue problem, not a
style preference.

## The banned centroid

**Cold-outreach tells (auto-reject):** "Quick question", "I hope this email finds you well",
"I noticed that you…" followed by something scraped from their bio, "I'll keep this brief" (in a long
email), "Just following up / circling back", flattery-then-pivot ("Love what you're building at X!
Speaking of which…"), "would you be open to a quick 15-minute call?" as the close of an otherwise
generic email (a small, specific ask is fine — even a 15-minute one — when a real observation earlier
in the message earned it), feature dumps,
and fake personalization — one swapped noun in a template. If the same email works with the company
name changed, it isn't outreach; it's spam with better grammar.

**Marketing-copy tells (auto-reject):** unlock / unleash / supercharge / effortless / seamless /
game-changing / revolutionize; "Say goodbye to X, say hello to Y"; rhetorical triads on autopilot
("faster, smarter, better"); benefit listicles pre-phrased for icon grids; exclamation-mark
enthusiasm. Thai copy has its own centroid — ban the same *moves*, not just the words:
"ปลดล็อกศักยภาพ", "ยกระดับ…อย่างไร้รอยต่อ", "ครบ จบ ในที่เดียว" as reflexes.

## What replaces it

1. **Write from their world, not yours.** The Mom Test discipline (`solo-sell`) applied to copy: open
   with the trigger, the moment the pain last bit, or their own words captured in discovery — not with
   your product. One specific observation that proves you actually looked beats three paragraphs of
   value proposition.
   - Slop: *"Hi Khun A — quick question. I noticed you run a clinic. Our AI-powered platform helps
     clinics streamline bookings seamlessly. Open to a quick 15-minute call?"*
   - Sharp: *"เห็นโพสต์ในกลุ่มเจ้าของคลินิกว่าคุณตอบจอง LINE เองถึงห้าทุ่ม — ผมทำตัวจัดคิวที่อ่านแชท LINE
     ให้แทน คลินิกที่เชียงใหม่ใช้แล้วงานตอบแชทหายไปวันละ ~2 ชม. เล่าให้ฟัง 5 นาทีไหมครับ"*
   The sharp one contains a real observation, a real number, and an ask sized to the relationship.
2. **The read-aloud test.** Would the founder say this sentence, out loud, to the prospect's face?
   If it would be embarrassing spoken, it's embarrassing written. Copy should sound like the founder
   messaging a peer — because for a solo business, that's literally what it is.
3. **Personalization is real or absent.** If no genuine detail about the prospect is known, don't
   fabricate one (grounding rule) — write a shorter, honest email instead. A specific email to the
   right stranger beats a fake-personal email to anyone.
4. **Each draft earns its own anchor.** When drafting a batch, every message anchors to a different
   real detail per prospect. Producing the same email with nouns swapped violates the whole point of
   founder-led outreach.

## Templates are floors, not molds

The per-skill output blocks (GTM plan, playbook, funnel spec) define minimum fields, not prose shape.
Lead with the sharpest thing this run found — the channel insight, the objection pattern, the leak in
the funnel — then the structured block. Two founders' plans should not read like one document with
the businesses swapped.

## Where this bites, per skill

- **solo-distribute** — build-in-public posts and content pass the read-aloud test; no
  engagement-bait formats ("I made $0 for 6 months. Then everything changed. A thread 🧵") unless the
  founder's actual voice is that.
- **solo-sell** — every outreach draft passes the banned-tells list and anchors to a real prospect
  detail; objection answers are written in the prospect's captured language, not sales-book language.
- **solo-grow** — landing/funnel copy and lifecycle emails pass the marketing-tells list; lifecycle
  emails read like a helpful human wrote them once, not like a drip tool generated them forever.
- **solo-model** — the offer is stated in buyer-world outcomes ("get your evenings back") with real
  numbers, not category language ("a comprehensive booking solution").
