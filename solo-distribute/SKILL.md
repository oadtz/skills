# Solo — Distribute (get the first customers, build a channel)

Take a chosen model (from `solo-model`) and turn "I built it / I'm building it" into **a repeatable
way for the right people to find it and pay.** Output is a concrete go-to-market plan: one primary
channel, the audience-building motion that runs alongside the product, a first-customers play, and a
weekly cadence one person can keep.

## The governing principle (read first)

**Distribution is the product's twin, not its afterthought — and one channel done deeply beats five
done shallowly.**

The single most common reason solo products fail is not a bad product — it's that **no one finds
it**. Surveys of indie founders put distribution, not building, as the #1 problem (the large majority
cite it as the deciding factor). AI has made *building* cheap, which only makes distribution the
*more* scarce advantage. For a one-person team with finite hours, that means three things, all the
same principle:

1. **Start distribution before launch, not after.** Build an audience in parallel with the product
   (build-in-public, an email list, presence in the community where buyers already are) so launch day
   has someone to launch *to*. A product revealed to an empty room is the default failure.
2. **Go one channel deep.** A solo founder cannot run five channels well. Pick the *one* channel where
   the target buyer already gathers and that fits the founder's strengths, and get genuinely good at
   it before adding a second. Five shallow channels is just five ways to be invisible.
3. **Earn the first customers manually; systematize only what works.** The first 10–100 customers come
   from doing things that don't scale — direct outreach, showing up in communities, hand-holding.
   *Then* turn the motion that worked into a repeatable cadence. Don't automate before you have a
   working motion to automate.

Everything below is this principle applied to one decision.

## Host capability mapping

- **User input**: ask one concise question at a time; use structured choices if supported.
- **Research**: use the host's web/search/browser to find *where the specific buyer gathers* (which
  subreddits, communities, directories, search terms, marketplaces) and what comparable products do;
  use connected analytics/CRM if the user already has traffic or a list.
- **No live research**: reason from the buyer profile in the model, but mark channel-fit as a
  hypothesis to test with a small real experiment.
- **File output**: write the GTM plan to a file/artifact if supported; else deliver it in chat.

**Grounding:** before stating any external fact (where the buyer gathers, channel/conversion
benchmarks, "what's working now" tactics, tool/platform facts), follow `../solo-grounding.md` if
present — source-or-tag every claim, re-fetch dated stats, and treat channel-fit and
willingness-to-pay as behavior-backed hypotheses (Mom Test), not stated intent. It does not make you
less decisive about the one channel you pick.

## Workflow

Four steps: **Pin the buyer & where they are → Pick ONE channel → Design the audience + first-
customers play → Set a weekly cadence.**

### Step 1 — Pin the buyer and where they already gather

Pull the buyer from `solo-model` (don't re-derive). Then locate them concretely: *which* communities,
search queries, marketplaces, newsletters, events, or people they already pay attention to. "Founders"
is not a location; "the r/X subreddit, the Y Slack, and people searching 'Z tool'" is. Channel choice
falls out of *where the buyer is* + *what the founder can sustain*.

### Step 2 — Pick ONE primary channel

Read `references/channel-playbook.md`. Choose **one** primary channel with a one-line rationale,
matched to both the buyer's location and the founder's strengths (writing vs talking vs building vs
SEO vs ads vs direct sales). Name a likely second channel for *later* — but commit to one now.
Resist the urge to "do a bit of everything"; that's the failure mode this principle exists to prevent.

### Step 3 — Design the audience motion + the first-customers play

Two pieces, run together:

- **Audience motion (compounding):** the parallel, ongoing motion that builds owned reach over time —
  e.g. build-in-public posts + an email list, SEO content on the buyer's queries, a niche newsletter,
  or consistent presence/help in the buyer's community. The asset to *own* is usually an email list,
  because it's the one channel an algorithm can't take away.
- **First-customers play (manual, now):** the do-things-that-don't-scale push for the first 10–100 —
  direct outreach to people with the problem, showing up where they ask questions and genuinely
  helping, a focused launch (community/marketplace/Product-Hunt-style), or pre-selling to the list.
  Hand pre-sales back to `solo-fund` if the goal is also to fund the build.

### Step 4 — Set a sustainable weekly cadence

Distribution for a solo founder is lost or won on *consistency*, and consistency is a sustainability
problem (see `solo-sustain`). Define a concrete weekly cadence the user will actually keep — e.g. "3
build-in-public posts + 5 direct outreaches + 1 piece of content per week" — small enough to sustain
for months, because compounding only pays if you don't quit. Then present the plan:

> **Buyer & where**: [buyer] gathers at [specific places] · **Primary channel**: [one] (because […])
> **Audience motion**: [compounding motion, owned asset = usually email list]
> **First-customers play**: [manual play for first 10–100]
> **Weekly cadence**: [concrete, sustainable] · **Second channel (later)**: [name]
>
> Want me to hand this to **solo-sell** to convert the attention into closed, paying deals
> (founder-led sales), to **solo-sustain** to make this cadence runnable long-term without burning out,
> or back to **solo-fund** to pre-sell to the audience you build?

The user commits to the channel and cadence before moving on.

## Operating principles

All of these are the governing principle applied — not a flat checklist.

- **Distribution is a first-class workstream, equal to building.** Schedule it; don't treat it as
  what's left over after the code. AI making the build cheap raises distribution's relative value.
- **One channel, deep.** Mastery of one beats dabbling in five. Add a second only once the first is
  working and repeatable.
- **Own your audience.** Followers on a platform are rented; an email list is owned. Bias the
  audience motion toward an asset no algorithm can revoke.
- **Do things that don't scale first.** Manual outreach and hands-on help win the first customers and
  teach you the real message — systematize *after* you've found what works, not before.
- **Consistency over intensity.** A small cadence sustained for a year beats a launch-week sprint then
  silence. Set the bar where the founder won't quit (this is why `solo-sustain` is the next stage).
- **Honesty:** if the chosen channel doesn't actually reach the buyer, or the cadence isn't realistic
  for one person's hours, say so — a plausible-sounding plan the founder can't sustain is worse than a
  smaller one they can. Treat channel-fit as a hypothesis until a small real test confirms it.

## Execution

**Don't stop at the plan — execute it.** Once the founder has made the decisions this skill is built
around, build the real deliverables with the right tool (draft the actual content calendar, posts, SEO
outlines, and outreach messages ready to use) instead of handing back advice. Stage anything that posts
publicly, acts from the founder's accounts, or spends money for their one-click approval. Full contract:
`../solo-execution.md`.
