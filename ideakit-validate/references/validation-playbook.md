# Decision-driven validation playbook

Use this in `ideakit-validate` after the maturity gate. Validation should resolve a decision, not fill
eight standard research sections. Select dimensions whose answers can change go/reframe/park/kill.

## Evidence discipline

Maintain an evidence ledger with **Observed / Inferred / Bet** labels. Cite observations beside the
claim; write the inference bridge yourself; make bets falsifiable. Search for counter-evidence and
failed attempts, not only confirmation. Current behavior, spend, workarounds, and commitments outweigh
hypothetical enthusiasm.

Use an evidence ladder: **E0 thesis/desk signal → E1 observed behavior → E2 costly commitment → E3
transaction or sustained real use → E4 repeated independent transactions/behavior**. Report the
highest level earned for each critical dependency. A concept does not become “validated” merely
because competitor research and market statistics look favorable.

Search depth follows uncertainty and stakes. A narrow low-cost experiment may need 3–5 targeted
searches; a capital-intensive, regulated, or dependency-heavy venture may need 15–30 plus first-party
interviews or data. Search count is not a quality metric.

## Research dimensions

### Demand and behavior

Investigate triggering situations, frequency, stakes, current workaround, non-consumption, switching
forces, and evidence of commitment. Use complaints carefully: attention is not willingness to change
or pay.

### Alternatives and category history

Find direct competitors, substitutes, manual/internal solutions, doing nothing, and prior failed
attempts. Capture what users praise, reject, and tolerate; business model and distribution; and why
the gap may persist.

### Distribution and trust

Determine how the first 10 users are specifically reachable, who already has their attention, what
trust is required, sales cycle or adoption friction, and whether the proposed channel fits the buyer.
“Content”, “partnerships”, and “go viral” are not distribution plans.

### Economics and buyer

Identify buyer, budget or value pool, comparable spend, pricing pattern, value created, margin or
operational load, and affordable-loss path. Market-size headlines are weak evidence unless the segment
and reachable share match the wedge.

### Execution and dependencies

Research technical or operational hard parts, required capabilities, supply/partner constraints,
regulation, build-vs-buy, and founder resource limits. Estimate only at the resolution needed for the
decision.

### Platform or ecosystem replication

Use when a platform, model provider, marketplace, supplier, policy, or distribution gatekeeper controls
the wedge. For AI/software, inspect relevant official changelogs, roadmaps, extension ecosystems, and
open protocols. For other ventures, inspect their actual dependency owner. Ask what survives if the
gatekeeper absorbs, bans, copies, or reprices the feature. Do not default to an AI-platform table.

### Timing and trajectory

Select only forces capable of changing the venture: capability/cost curve, regulation, infrastructure,
culture, demographics, funding, buyer behavior, or incumbent response. Create scenarios only when they
change the decision; avoid speculative technology laundry lists.

### Compounding power

Test whether the wedge can plausibly accumulate network, scale, switching cost, brand, cornered
resource, process power, or counter-positioning. A day-one moat is not required; a believable path from
wedge to durable advantage is.

## Synthesis

Return:

1. the strongest disconfirming finding;
2. findings organized by selected dimension;
3. evidence ledger with sources and counter-signals;
4. what changed in the thesis;
5. go/reframe/park/kill recommendation;
6. critical assumptions and affordable-loss tests;
7. research gaps that remain material.
8. evidence level earned and the next costly signal required.

Insufficient validation includes unsupported pricing/demand claims, no meaningful counter-search,
generic distribution, ignored critical dependencies, or a recommendation that does not change when
the evidence changes.
