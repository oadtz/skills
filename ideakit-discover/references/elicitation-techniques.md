# Elicitation techniques — how to excavate a direction from a person

These are the named methods behind Steps 2–4 of `ideakit-discover`. They share one root: **a person at
zero cannot answer "what do you want to build?" directly, and will confabulate if pushed — so you pull
the answer from evidence and reaction, never from a poll.** Use them as tools, not a script; follow the
energy and drill where it lights up.

## 1. The Mom Test, turned inward

Rob Fitzpatrick's *Mom Test*: never ask people for opinions about the future ("would you use this?",
"do you want this?") — they'll lie to be nice or to sound good, even to themselves. Ask about
**specific past behavior** instead, because behavior is the only honest signal. Turned inward, on the
founder:

- Ask **what they've actually done**, not what they think they'd like: hours spent, money spent, things
  taught to themselves, rabbit holes, side projects abandoned and why, communities they lurk in.
- Anchor every future-facing question in a real past: not "what are you passionate about?" (invites
  confabulation) but "what's something you spent a whole weekend on that nobody asked you to?"
- Discount stated interests that have **no behavioral trail**. "I'm interested in AI" with zero
  evidence is noise; "I rebuilt my budget in a spreadsheet three times for fun" is signal.

**Tell:** if an answer is about the future or an identity claim ("I'm a creative person"), reach for
the past behind it ("when did that last show up — what did you actually make?").

## 2. Laddering (means–end)

A technique from psychology and UX research for climbing from a surface preference to the **value**
underneath. Ask "why does that matter to you?" repeatedly (usually 2–3 rungs) until you hit something
stable that won't ladder further — autonomy, mastery, freedom, helping a specific group, status,
curiosity, security, legacy.

```
"I liked building the Discord bot"   →  why?
"People actually used it"            →  why did that matter?
"I made something that helped them"  →  why does that matter to you?
"I want my work to be useful to a community I'm part of"   ← stable value (the compass)
```

The surface answer (a Discord bot) is just *where* the value showed up. The value is what should steer
the direction — and it generalizes to many possible ideas, which is exactly what you want to hand to
generate. Don't ladder everything; ladder the 2–3 things that visibly energized them.

## 3. Projective prompts (loosen latent wants)

People guard their real wants behind "be realistic." Projective prompts remove the constraint so the
latent want can surface, then you bring it back to earth:

- *"If you had a fully funded year and literally couldn't fail, what would you secretly try?"*
- *"What would you build just for yourself, even if no one ever paid for it?"*
- *"Whose job or business do you quietly envy — and what specifically about it?"*
- *"Fast-forward 5 years to a version of your life you're proud of — what are you spending your days
  doing?"*

Treat the answer as a **direction signal, not a literal plan**. The point is to find the shape of what
they want (autonomy? making? teaching? a particular audience?), which you then ground against their
real constraints in Step 3.

## 4. React-to-concretes (recognition beats generation)

The single biggest lever for someone at zero. People are bad at generating from a blank page and
**excellent at reacting** — "that, not that" is instant and honest. So stop asking them to invent:

- Put **3–5 deliberately different concrete options** in front of them — real example products,
  niches, founder stories, or "a day in the life" of a path. Fetch real ones if research is available;
  describe them in text if not. Span different *shapes* (a maker/product, a productized service, an
  audience/creator path, a tool/API, a community) so the contrast is structural, not cosmetic.
- Read **attraction and recoil equally** — what they recoil from rules out a whole region cheaply.
- Mine their **edits**: "that, but for nurses instead of devs" or "that, without the client work" is
  the person designing their own direction. Capture the edit verbatim; it's often the real answer.

This is the step that makes discovery work where a questionnaire fails — the signal comes from
*reaction to something real*, not self-report.

## 5. Critical Incident probing

When a topic lights up, zoom into **one specific moment** rather than generalities. "Tell me about the
last time you [felt that / built that / got frustrated by that] — walk me through it." Concrete
incidents carry the texture (who, what, why it mattered) that abstractions hide, and they're hard to
fake. This is the same discipline as the Mom Test's "concrete example from last week," applied to
mining interests and frustrations.

## 6. Energy audit

Direction has to sit on the energizing side of the ledger or it won't survive contact with the long
slog of building. Separate two things that look alike:

- **Lose-track-of-time work** — what they do where hours vanish. Build *toward* this.
- **Good-but-draining work** — things they're competent at but that cost energy. People often default
  to building around a marketable skill that quietly drains them; flag it, don't build the core on it.

Ask for both explicitly. A direction that's all "drain" with high pay is a burnout machine (and the
`solo-sustain` skill will tell you the same thing later).

## Putting it together

A good session usually runs: **Mom-Test the past → energy-audit it → ladder the hot spots to values →
projective prompt to surface the hidden want → react-to-concretes to sharpen it.** You won't use every
technique every time. The output you're driving toward is the **edge map** (see
`edge-map-template.md`): a portrait grounded in evidence, not a list of things the person claimed to
like.

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**
- **Polling.** "What are you passionate about? What do you want to build?" — produces confabulation.
- **Leading.** Feeding a want and accepting agreement. Ask open, then stay quiet.
- **Therapizing.** This is a buildable-direction intake, not counseling. Stay warm but on-task; don't
  over-interpret, and don't pry into sensitive history the direction doesn't need.
- **Jumping to ideas.** The moment you start pitching specific products, you've skipped to generate's
  job and abandoned the person. Hand off the frame instead.
