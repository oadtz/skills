# Audience playbooks — what makes each one act

Read this in Step 1. One averaged message rarely helps several audiences decide — pick the primary
audience and tune to its actual decision. The three core audiences want fundamentally
different things.

## Customer — "this is for me, and it ends my pain"

What they feel before acting: recognition ("they get my exact problem") then relief/desire ("I can
have that better life").

- **Make them the hero, not you.** The customer is the protagonist with a problem; your idea is the
  guide that hands them a plan and a win. The story is about *their* transformation.
- **Lead with the pain they live in**, described so precisely they think "have you been watching
  me?" Specifics earn trust; generic benefits don't.
- **Show the after-state as a feeling, not a feature.** Not "automated reconciliation" — "you stop
  dreading the monthly audit because the numbers already reconcile themselves."
- **One outcome, one next step.** A single clear CTA ("start with one dataset, free").
- Proof that lowers risk: a similar customer's result, a number, a guarantee.

What deflates it: talking about your company, your tech stack, your funding. They don't care yet.

## Investor — “the upside is large, the wedge is credible, and this team learns efficiently”

What they need before acting: a credible view of upside, evidence that the team can reach an initial
wedge, and clarity about what remains uncertain. They are underwriting a sequence of learning and
execution, not purchasing certainty.

- **Open with the strongest decision-relevant observation or contradiction.** Use a world shift only
  when current evidence shows one; a sharp customer behavior can be stronger.
- **Show consequence, not theatrical winners and losers.** Name who changes behavior, budget, or risk,
  and over what horizon.
- **Connect wedge to upside.** Show the specific future the venture could create, then the sequence of
  commitments and capabilities required to reach it.
- **Why now / why this mechanism / why this team.** Separate observed tailwinds, inferred advantage,
  and the bet investors are actually being asked to underwrite.
- **Asymmetric upside, honest risk.** Show the size of the prize and that you know the real risks —
  naming risks builds credibility, hiding them destroys it.

What deflates it: feature tours, a TAM slide with no reachable wedge, inevitability language,
“we have no competitors,” and unsupported numbers.

## Teammate / recruit — "a mission that matters and is winnable, with a real role for me"

What they feel before acting: meaning + belonging + the thrill of a winnable adventure. People leave
safe jobs for missions, not paychecks.

- **Lead with the why** — the meaningful change you're making in the world, and why it matters to a
  human being, not a market.
- **Make it feel winnable.** Mission without a credible path is a dream; show the wedge, the early
  signal, the plan that makes a smart skeptic believe it can actually happen.
- **Give them a role and ownership.** Not "we need a backend engineer" — "you'd own the thing the
  whole product depends on, and shape it from zero." People join to matter.
- **Be honest about the hard parts.** The right teammates are drawn to a real, hard, winnable
  challenge — not a frictionless fantasy. Candor about difficulty is a feature.
- The adventure framing: "join something early, hard, and important" beats "join a stable company."

What deflates it: leading with perks/comp, vague mission with no path, or overselling certainty.

## A note on mixed audiences

If you truly must address several at once (e.g., a launch that reaches customers and investors),
build for the primary one and add a thin layer for the secondary — never average them into a bland
message that moves nobody. When in doubt, ask the user who, if only one person in the room had to
act, that person is.
