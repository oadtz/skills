# Pricing playbook for solo founders

The principle applied to price: *your marginal cost is ~zero and your time is the scarce input, so
price on the value delivered and the buyer's budget — never on your cost.*

## The five rules

1. **Price on value and on who pays — not on cost.** Cost-plus is the classic solo error: with
   near-zero marginal cost it underprices drastically. Ask "what is this worth to the buyer / what
   does the problem cost them?" and price against that.
2. **Start higher than feels comfortable.** Underpricing is the more common and more damaging mistake
   for solo founders. Low prices attract the most demanding, highest-support customers (the worst
   possible mix for one person) and starve runway. You can always discount; raising prices on
   existing customers is painful.
3. **Decouple revenue from your hours.** Prefer flat / tiered / per-seat / usage pricing over pure
   hourly, so income isn't hard-capped by one calendar. Even a productized service should be priced
   per *outcome/package*, not per hour.
4. **Let the buyer type set the floor.** B2B tolerates far higher prices than B2C — a $200/mo tool is
   an easy business expense; a $200/mo consumer app is a hard sell. If you can serve a business buyer,
   the same effort earns multiples more. Documented solo SaaS sweet spot: ~$29–$199/mo for SMB/B2B.
5. **Make the tiers do the selling.** A simple good/better/best with an obvious "most pick this"
   middle tier outperforms a single price. Anchor with a high tier; don't bury your best plan.

## Starting-price heuristics by model

- **Productized service:** price per package at the value of the outcome; start at the high end of
  comparable freelancer/agency quotes, since "fixed scope, fast, predictable" is worth a premium.
- **MicroSaaS:** anchor to what the buyer pays for the tool/process you replace; default to monthly
  tiers in the ~$29–$199/mo SMB band; offer annual (discounted) to improve cashflow and cut churn.
- **Digital product:** price by transformation, not page count — a focused template/course that saves
  a pro real hours can command far more than "ebook" pricing; bundle for a higher anchor.
- **Usage/API:** a free or cheap entry tier to drive self-serve adoption, then meter the value metric
  that grows with the customer's success.

## Common traps

- **Free-forever to "get users first."** Free users don't validate a *business*; you only learn the
  real price by charging. Charge from day one (a trial or a cheap paid tier counts).
- **Discounting instead of narrowing.** If buyers balk, the fix is usually a sharper niche or clearer
  value, not a lower price.
- **One price, no anchor.** Without a higher tier to compare against, your price looks expensive in
  isolation.
- **Hourly creep.** Billing time re-caps your income at your hours and punishes you for getting
  faster — the opposite of leverage.

## How to actually test price

Don't theorize past this skill — route the test to `solo-distribute`: pre-sell at the intended price,
or put up a pricing page and watch real conversion. Treat every pre-stated willingness-to-pay as an
assumption until money changes hands (people overstate what they'd pay; weight what they *do*).
