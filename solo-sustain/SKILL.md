# Solo — Sustain (run it solo, for the long haul)

Take a working (or soon-working) solo business and make it **something one person can run for years
without breaking the person.** Output is a concrete operating plan: what to automate or systematize,
the calm-company defaults to adopt, and the guardrails that protect the founder's time, energy, and
finances — so the business compounds instead of collapsing.

## The governing principle (read first)

**The founder is the single point of failure and the scarcest asset — protect the asset, and buy
leverage through systems before headcount.**

In a one-person business, the founder is simultaneously the product team, support, sales, ops, and
finance — and the *only* irreplaceable component. Research on solo founders is blunt about it: the
strongest predictor of failure is not strategy but **burnout** (recent surveys put burnout rates
above half and anxiety far higher). So longevity isn't a soft topic — it's the core operational risk.
Three consequences, all the same principle:

1. **Leverage beats effort; systems beat headcount.** The solo way to scale output is automation,
   templates, self-serve, and tooling (now hugely amplified by AI) — *not* working more hours and
   *not* reflexively hiring. Headcount is the most expensive, least reversible "feature" you can add
   (see `forge-architect`'s "record irreversibly"); reach for systems first, people last and
   deliberately.
2. **Protect the asset on purpose.** Sustainable hours, real time off, boundaries on support and
   scope, and an emergency/"hit by a bus" plan are not indulgences — they're uptime for the only
   component that can't be replaced. A calm, profitable business that lasts ten years beats a
   hyper-growth sprint that ends in a burnt-out founder fire-selling.
3. **Calm-company defaults, not startup-theatre defaults.** Grow at the pace profit allows; say no to
   work, customers, and features that add disproportionate operational load; optimize for durable
   profit and the founder's life, not vanity growth. This is a deliberate stance against the
   hyper-scale defaults that don't fit a solo business.

Everything below is this principle applied to one decision.

## A duty of care (read before the workflow)

This skill touches the founder's wellbeing. Hold these always:

- **Never optimize the business at the expense of the person.** Do not propose, endorse, or "respect
  their hustle" on sleep deprivation, skipped meals/healthcare, no days off, or "grind now, recover
  later." If the user frames overwork as the plan, gently push back and treat sustainability as the
  goal, not a nice-to-have.
- **Take signs of burnout seriously.** Exhaustion, dread, hopelessness, isolation, or "I can't stop or
  it all falls apart" are operational red flags *and* human ones. Name them with care, normalize
  resting, and — if there are signs of real distress — suggest talking to a professional or someone
  they trust. Don't run a clinical assessment; respond as a thoughtful collaborator who cares.
- **The point of a calm company is a good life.** Keep that the frame.

## Workflow

Four steps: **Map where the time goes → Buy leverage (systems before headcount) → Set calm-company
defaults → Put up personal guardrails.**

### Step 1 — Map where the founder's time and energy actually go

Get a real picture of the week: what recurring work eats the hours (support, manual onboarding,
fulfillment, bookkeeping, content, sales), and what *drains* the founder regardless of hours. Separate
**high-leverage work only the founder can do** (product judgment, key relationships, the core craft)
from **dilutive recurring work** that should be removed. You can't fix load you haven't named.

### Step 2 — Buy leverage: systems before headcount

Read `references/automation-and-burnout.md`. For each pile of dilutive recurring work, climb a
leverage ladder — cheapest, most reversible first:

1. **Eliminate** — does this task need to exist? (Narrow scope, kill a feature, drop a needy customer
   segment.)
2. **Self-serve / deflect** — docs, FAQs, onboarding that removes the question, defaults that prevent
   the ticket.
3. **Automate** — scripts, no-code/workflow tools, and AI for support drafts, content, ops, and
   admin. This is the solo founder's primary lever and the one AI has expanded most.
4. **Template & systematize** — turn the rest into runbooks so it's fast and consistent (and one day
   delegable).
5. **Delegate to *services* before people** — contractors/VAs/agencies for bounded tasks before any
   employee.
6. **Hire — last, deliberate, and reversible-first.** Only when there's durable revenue to support it
   and the role removes a clear, persistent bottleneck. Prefer fractional/contract before full-time.
   Treat a first hire like an irreversible architectural decision, not a reflex.

Produce a short **leverage list**: the top recurring drains, and for each the lowest rung that
removes it.

### Step 3 — Set calm-company operating defaults

Adopt explicit defaults that keep the business one-person-runnable: grow at the pace profit allows;
default to *no* on work/customers/features that add disproportionate operational load; keep the
product surface small (every feature is forever-support); prefer async, low-touch revenue; price so
you serve *fewer, better* customers rather than many needy ones. Write these down as the business's
operating stance so future decisions have a yardstick.

### Step 4 — Put up personal guardrails (protect the asset)

Set concrete, humane guardrails — and keep them measurable so they're real:

- **Sustainable hours + real time off** (and actual coverage/auto-responder so time off is genuine).
- **Boundaries**: support hours, response-time expectations, scope limits — published, not heroic.
- **Financial buffer**: a personal runway/emergency cushion so the business never forces unhealthy
  choices; pay yourself deliberately.
- **Continuity / "bus factor"**: minimal docs, access, and a plan so a sick week doesn't sink the
  business — which also lowers the constant background anxiety.
- **Energy, not just time**: protect focus blocks for the high-leverage work only the founder can do.

Then present the plan:

> **Top time-drains**: [list] · **Leverage moves**: [task → lowest rung that removes it]
> **Calm-company defaults**: [the explicit operating stances]
> **Guardrails**: hours […], boundaries […], buffer […], continuity […]
> **Hire?**: [not yet / only when X] — systems first.
>
> Revisit this loop whenever operating load creeps back up. If the load is really coming from the
> *model*, loop back to **solo-model**.

The user commits to the operating plan and guardrails before finishing.

## Operating principles

All of these are the governing principle applied — not a flat checklist.

- **Leverage over hours.** The answer to "too much work" is almost never "more hours" — it's
  eliminate → self-serve → automate → template → delegate, in that order.
- **Systems before headcount.** Hiring is the most expensive, least reversible move; exhaust systems
  and services first, and treat a first hire as deliberate and reversible-first.
- **Small surface, low touch.** Every feature and every needy customer is permanent operational load.
  Defend simplicity; saying no is an operations strategy.
- **Calm and profitable beats big and brittle.** Durable profit + a sustainable founder compounds for
  years; a hyper-growth sprint that burns the founder out ends the business.
- **Protect the person first.** Never trade the founder's health for output. A sustainability plan
  that quietly assumes overwork isn't a plan — it's the failure mode with extra steps.
- **Honesty:** if the business genuinely can't be run solo at its current shape, say so — the answer
  may be to shrink scope, raise prices to serve fewer customers, or rethink the model, not to grind
  harder.

## Execution

**Don't stop at the plan — execute it.** Build the real deliverables with the right tool (write the
actual runbooks, SOPs, templates, and automation scripts that remove the recurring work) instead of
handing back a list of what to automate. Stage anything that spends money, acts from the founder's
accounts, or is irreversible for their one-click approval. Full contract: `../solo-execution.md`.
