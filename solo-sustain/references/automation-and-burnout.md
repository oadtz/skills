# Leverage, calm-company defaults, and anti-burnout guardrails

The principle: *the founder is the single point of failure and the scarcest asset — protect it, and
buy leverage through systems before headcount.* This file details the three tools: the leverage
ladder, calm-company defaults, and personal guardrails.

## The leverage ladder (cheapest, most reversible first)

For every pile of recurring work, climb from the top. Don't jump to "hire" when "eliminate" or
"automate" would do — that's how solo businesses acquire cost and fragility they didn't need.

1. **Eliminate.** Does this work need to exist at all? The cheapest task is the one you deleted. Narrow
   the product surface, kill a low-value feature, drop the customer segment that generates most of the
   support. Saying no removes work permanently.
2. **Self-serve / deflect.** Turn recurring questions into docs, FAQs, tooltips, and onboarding that
   prevents the ticket. Design defaults so the problem never reaches you. The best support ticket is
   the one that never gets filed.
3. **Automate.** Scripts, no-code/workflow automation, and AI are the solo founder's primary lever —
   and the one AI has expanded most:
   - *Support*: AI-drafted replies, canned responses, a help bot over your docs.
   - *Content/marketing*: repurposing, drafting, scheduling (keep the founder's voice and judgment).
   - *Ops/admin*: billing, invoicing, reminders, onboarding emails, data hygiene on autopilot.
   - *Product*: cron jobs and pipelines instead of manual steps.
   Rule: automate the *repetitive and rule-based*; keep human judgment where it's the actual value.
4. **Template & systematize.** Whatever's left, turn into runbooks/checklists so it's fast, consistent,
   and — crucially — *delegable later without you*. Systems are what make a one-person business sellable
   and survivable.
5. **Delegate to services before people.** Contractors, VAs, agencies, freelancers for *bounded* tasks
   (bookkeeping, design, a support shift). Variable cost, low commitment, reversible — the right step
   before any employee.
6. **Hire — last, deliberate, reversible-first.** Only when there's durable revenue to fund it *and*
   the role removes a clear, persistent bottleneck that systems and services can't. Prefer
   fractional/part-time/contract before full-time. A first hire reshapes the business (management
   load, payroll obligation, less reversibility) — treat it like an irreversible architectural
   decision, not a reflex when you're tired. "I'm overwhelmed" usually means "automate/eliminate
   more," not "hire."

## Calm-company defaults (37signals-style, adapted for solo)

Adopt explicitly so they act as a yardstick for future decisions:

- **Grow at the pace profit allows.** No growth mandate you didn't choose; profitability over vanity
  scale.
- **Default to no.** New work, custom requests, edge-case features, and high-maintenance customers all
  add permanent operational load. The default answer is no unless it clearly earns its keep.
- **Keep the surface small.** Every feature is forever-support and forever-maintenance. Simplicity is
  an operations strategy, not just a design one.
- **Serve fewer, better customers.** Price and position to attract low-touch, high-value customers
  rather than many needy, price-sensitive ones (set this up back in `solo-model`).
- **Async and low-touch by default.** Prefer revenue and support models that don't require the founder
  live and in-the-loop.

## Anti-burnout guardrails (humane and measurable)

Burnout is the #1 documented predictor of solo-founder failure — these are uptime for the
irreplaceable component, not indulgences. Make each concrete:

- **Sustainable hours + real time off.** Define the working week and protect it. Set up coverage /
  auto-responders / status pages so time off is *actually* off. Rest is maintenance, not reward.
- **Published boundaries.** Support hours, realistic response-time expectations, and scope limits —
  stated to customers, not silently absorbed through heroics.
- **Financial buffer.** A personal emergency cushion / runway so the business never forces unhealthy
  choices. Pay yourself deliberately; an underpaid founder is a burnout risk on a timer.
- **Continuity / bus factor.** Minimal docs, password/access management, and a "if I'm sick for a
  week" plan. This both protects the business and reduces the constant background dread that fuels
  burnout.
- **Protect energy, not just time.** Reserve focus blocks for the high-leverage work only the founder
  can do; batch shallow work; guard against the context-switching tax of being every department at once.
- **Stay connected.** Isolation amplifies solo-founder stress — peer communities, a mastermind, or
  founder friends are a real operational safeguard, not a soft extra.

## When sustainability is actually a model problem

If no amount of automation makes the business one-person-runnable — the model is inherently
high-touch, or success simply means linearly more founder-hours — that's a signal to loop back to
`solo-model`: narrow scope, raise prices to serve fewer customers, or change the model. Grinding
harder is not the fix, and never at the cost of the founder's health.
